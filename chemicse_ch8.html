The Periodic Table

INTRODUCTION

Elements are pure substances made up of one kind of atoms and cannot be broken down into simpler substances.There are today about 118 elements discovered so far.

CLASSIFICATION OF ELEMENTS

1. Early Chemists - attempted to classify elements on the basis of their properties, nature, character, valency, etc..

2. Dobereiner's Triads (1815) - arranged the elements in increasing order of their atomic weights into well marked groups of three called 'triads' in which the atomic weight of the middle element was found to be generally the arithmatic mean of the atomic weights of the other two elements.
Method did not hold true for all elements.
Properties of elements grouped into triads were found not to be similar.

3. Newland's law of Octaves (1864) - arranged the elements in increasing order of their atomic weights showing resemblance in physical and chemical properties between the eighth element and the first element.
Method showed odd grouping and failed to leave spaces for undiscovered elements.
Method of classification showed meaning with light metals e.g.- Li and Na but failed with heavy metals beyond K.

4. Mendeleeff's Contribution (1869) - arranged the elements in increasing order of their atomic weights in the form of a table called periodic table and stated that - 'Properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic weights'.
Elements were arranged in horizontal rows called 'periods' and vertical columns called 'groups'.
Elements similar with respect to their chemical properties are grouped together and have atomic weights of nearly the same value.
Elements in the same group had the same 'valency' and similar chemical properties.
A number of gaps were left in the table for undiscovered elements.
Properties of undiscovered elements were predicted.
Incorrect atomic weights of some of the arranged elements were corrected with the knowledge of the atomic weights of the adjacent elements.

Defects
i. Certain pairs of elements having higher atomic weights have been given positions before the elements having lower atomic weights. e.g.- Co[at. wt. 58.9 at number 27] was placed before Ni[at. wt. 58.6 at number 28].
ii. Position of rare earths and actinides - could be justified only if arranged according to atomic number.
iii. Isotopes had to be placed at the same position according to atomic number.

5. Moseley's Classification - Modern Periodic Table (1912)
Modern Periodic Law - The physical and chemical properties of elements are periodic functions of their 'atomic numbers'.
Elements when arranged in increasing order of their atomic number, showed similarity in properties after regular intervals because recurrence of similar electronic configuration takes place.
Elements arranged in 'seven' horizontal rows called - 'Periods' and 'eighteen' vertical columns called 'Groups'.
Each period begins with an element having - one electron in the outermost shell and ends with completely filled outer shell elements.
Across a period is a transition from metallic to non-metallic character.
Groups 1 and 2 - are reactive metals
Groups 3 to 12 - are transition elements [metals]
Groups 13 to 17 - contain non-metals in the upper right hand corner
Group 18 - contains noble or inert gases


Fig: The Modern Periodic Table

PERIODS

Seven horizontal rows of elements arranged in increasing order of atomic number.
Period nos. 1,2,3,4,5,6 or 7 signifies number of electron shells of an element.
On moving left to right across a period:
i. Number of electron shells remain same.
ii. Valence electrons increases by one.
iii. There is transition from metallic to non-metallic character

GROUPS

Eighteen vertical columns with eight main groups.
Group nos. signify the number of Valence electrons of an element.
On moving down a subgroup:
i. Valence electrons remain same
ii. Chemical properties remain similar
iii. Number of electron shells increases by one
iv. metallic character increases down a subgroup

MAIN TYPES OF GROUPS IN THE PERIODIC TABLE

1. Group 1 [IA] - ALKALI METALS

Elements - Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr
Valence Electrons - one
Nature - Highly reactive, Electropositive- good reducing agents
Conduction - Good conductors of electricity
Reactivity - React with water forming strong alkalis

2. Group 2 [IIA] - ALKALINE EARTH METALS

Elements - Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra
Properties - similar to alkali metals

3. Group 3 to 12 [IB to VIIB & VIII] - TRANSITION ELEMENTS
They are all metals
They lie at the centre of the periodic table between Metals[highly electropositive] on the left and non-metals[highly electronegative] on the right.

INNER TRANSITION ELEMENTS - LANTHANIDE[RARE EARTHS] AND ACTINIDE SERIES
They lie at the bottom of the periodic table in two horizontal rows [14 elements each]
Lanthanide series - from Cerium 58Ce to Lutetium 71Lu [period-6]
Actinide series - from Thorium 90Th to Laurencium 103Lr [period-7]
They are all metals and highly reactive.

4. Groups 13 to 16 [IIIA to VIA] - POST TRANSITION ELEMENTS

Elements - Al to Ti; Ge to Pb; Sb, Bi, Po
They are weak metals and metalloids.

5. Group 17 [VIIA] - HALOGENS

Elements - F, Cl, Br, I, At
Valence Electrons - one
Nature - Highly reactive, Electronegative - good oxidising agents
Conduction - Non-conductors of electricity

6. Group 18 [0] - NOBLE GASES

Elements - He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn
Reactivity - stable electronic configuration, unreactive or inert (higher members form compounds under specific conditions.)

NORMAL ELEMENTS

Elements in which atoms have all shells complete except the outermost shell.
They consist of metals, non-metals and metalloids.
The no. of electrons in the outer-most shell signifies the group to which they belong.

BRIDGE ELEMENTS [Second and third period elements]

Elements of the second period show resemblance in properties with elements of the third period of the next group leading to a diagonal relationship.
Such elements are called bridge elements e.g.- Li & Mg; Be & Al; B & S.

FEATURES OF THE MODERN PERIODIC TABLE

i. The table is based on classification of elements using the fundamental property of atomic number.

The 1st period has elements H & He filling upto one and two electrons in their K-shell.
The 2nd period has elements - Li to Ne filling upto eight electrons in their L-shell.
The 3rd period has elements - Na to Ar filling upto eight electrons in their M-shell.
Thus, the complete periodic table of elements is easily and systematically built up.

ii. Easy segregation of elements in groups and periods.

iii. Separates Elements With respect To Nature Of Elements

Metals including alkali metals and alkaline earth metals are in the extreme left of the periodic table and transition elements are placed in the centre.
Non-metals are placed in the upper right hand corner of the modern periodic table.
Noble gases are placed in the extreme right of the periodic table.
Thus, the periodic table makes arrangement of elements easy and simple to reproduce.

Defects
Position of hydrogen which can be placed either in group 1 or group 17.
Position of lanthanides and actinides which do not have an individual place in the table.

GROUP CHARACTERISTICS OR PROPERTIES OF ELEMENTS IN A GROUP

Chemical properties depend on the no. of electrons an atom can gain, lose or share.
Elements in a group have similar or graded properties.

SOME GENERAL GROUP CHARACTERISTICS

ALLOTROPY

- Ability of an element to exist in more than one form.
Exhibited by elements of group 14 [except Pb], group 15 [except Bi], and group 16.

ELECTRONEGATIVITY

- Tendency of an atom to attract electrons towards itself when combined in a compound.
Non-metals gain electrons and are electronegative elements.
Metals lose electrons and are electropositive elements.
Electronegativity increases across a period and decreases down a group.
Non-metallic character increases across a period and decreases down a group.
